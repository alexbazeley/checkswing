## The bit, up front

Roughly every two years, some member of Congress introduces a bill to stop the
federal government from subsidizing billionaires' sports stadiums. It has a name
that tells you exactly what it does: the **No Tax Subsidies for Stadiums Act**. It
is bipartisan. It is cheap. It is popular with economists of every stripe, who
agree on almost nothing except that publicly financing pro sports venues is a bad
deal for the public.

And roughly every two years, it dies without a hearing, a markup, or a single
recorded vote.

It died in 2017 (S.1342 / H.R.811). It is dying right now, in the 119th Congress
(S.1192 / H.R.2434). The cause of death is always the same: it gets **referred to
a committee and never comes back out**. The Senate version goes to **Finance**.
The House version goes to **Ways and Means**. Those are the tax-writing committees
— because the stadium subsidy is a *tax* subsidy, the federal income-tax exemption
on the municipal bonds that cities and counties float to build the ballparks.

So we asked the CheckSwing question: **the MLB owners who benefit from that
subsidy — do they have a financial relationship with the members of the exact
committees where the bill to kill it goes to die?**

They do. And the honest shape of *how* they do is more damning than a bribe.

---

## Background: what the subsidy is, and why the bill keeps dying

- When a city or county issues municipal bonds to build a stadium, the interest on
  those bonds is generally **exempt from federal income tax**. That exemption is a
  federal subsidy — the U.S. Treasury eats the cost so the local deal pencils out.
  Economists across the spectrum have called it a giveaway with no public return.
- The **No Tax Subsidies for Stadiums Act** would close that exemption for pro
  sports venues. It has been introduced, in one chamber or another, in the 115th,
  116th, 117th, 118th, and 119th Congresses — by Republicans (Lankford, Russell,
  Grothman) and Democrats (Booker) alike.
- It has **never received a roll-call vote.** Every version has been referred to a
  tax-writing committee (Senate Finance / House Ways and Means) and left there.
- This is not a story about a vote. There is no vote. It is a story about the
  committees that decide whether a vote ever happens.

This matters right now for one owner in particular. **John Fisher's Athletics** are
building a Las Vegas ballpark with **up to $380 million in approved public
financing**, including roughly **$120–135 million in Clark County bonds** repaid
through a stadium tax district. Those are precisely the kind of municipal bonds
whose federal tax exemption the bill would end. Fisher is not a hypothetical
beneficiary of the stadium subsidy. He is, as of this writing, its single most
visible active user.

## The CheckSwing question — and the honest answer

We took every MLB principal owner in the archive, found their verified federal
donations to candidates, mapped each candidate to a sitting member of Congress,
and asked which of those members **currently sit on a committee that the 119th
Congress's stadium-subsidy bills were referred to** — Senate Finance and House
Ways and Means.

### What the data shows

- **Seven owners** in the archive have funded current members of the two tax-writing
  committees that hold the stadium bills — **18 donations totaling $64,500.**
- Ranked: **Ken Kendrick (Diamondbacks) $21,300**, **John Fisher (Athletics)
  $19,100**, **Tom Pohlad (Twins) $8,100**, **Jerry Reinsdorf (White Sox) $5,600**,
  **John Stanton (Mariners) $4,300**, **Steve Cohen (Mets) $3,300**, **Joe Pohlad
  (Twins) $2,800.**
- The recipients are exactly the gatekeepers you'd want if you liked the subsidy:
  members of **Senate Finance** (Republicans Cassidy, Tim Scott, Barrasso, Ron
  Johnson; Democrats Warner, Warnock, Smith) and **House Ways and Means** (Panetta,
  Schneider, Suozzi, Fischbach).
- The subsidy's biggest active beneficiary, **Fisher, put all $19,100 into Ways and
  Means** — the House committee that buried H.R.2434.

### What the data does *not* show — and we won't pretend otherwise

This is where the archive earns its keep. Four hard caveats, each of which cuts
against the easy headline:

1. **There is no vote to buy.** These bills die in committee; none ever reached the
   floor. Nothing here is a "purchased vote," and we make no such claim. The join
   is about *access to the room*, not a transaction on the record.
2. **The membership snapshot is current; the money is not.** Committee rosters here
   are the **present (119th Congress)** membership — the only rosters the source
   publishes. The donations span **2017 to 2025**. So this is "owners funded people
   who *now* sit on the committee that *now* holds the bill," **not** "owners paid
   them to handle this bill." Many of these gifts predate the current bills, and
   some predate the member's committee assignment. The join is deliberately fenced
   to current-Congress bills for exactly this reason, and you should read it as a
   map of *standing relationships*, not timed strikes.
3. **Fisher's money is a two-person relationship, not a committee strategy.** All
   $19,100 of Fisher's Ways-and-Means giving goes to just **two House Democrats —
   Jimmy Panetta (CA) and Brad Schneider (IL)** — the *same* California/Illinois
   cluster that turned up in our Save America's Pastime Act brief. This is a man
   re-upping the same handful of incumbent relationships, year after year, who
   happen to sit on a powerful committee. It is not evidence he targeted the stadium
   bill.
4. **Owners did not fund the bill's authors.** Across the entire lineage of the
   No Tax Subsidies for Stadiums Act, owner donations to any **sponsor or cosponsor**
   of the bills total **one gift — $6,600, from the Angelos family to a single
   cosponsor.** The people *trying to end* the subsidy get nothing. The people
   sitting on the committee that *parks* it get $64,500. Even the party split of
   that money — **$30,200 to Republicans, $34,300 to Democrats** — tells you it's
   chasing access on both sides of the aisle, not enforcing a partisan outcome.

### The real story is structural, and that's the point

Put the caveats together and you don't get a weaker story. You get a worse one.

The owners **don't have to target the stadium bill.** They don't have to buy a
vote, or pay the sponsors, or even know the bill exists. The subsidy survives on
**inertia** — referred to a committee, never scheduled, quietly dead — and the
owners who profit from it already maintain standing financial relationships with
the members of those committees, as a matter of routine incumbent giving. The bill
to end the subsidy needs someone on Finance or Ways and Means to *care enough to
move it.* The owners just need everyone to keep not caring. Money that buys nothing
in particular is buying exactly that.

Fisher is the cleanest illustration. He is financing a ballpark with the very
tax-exempt bonds the bill targets, and his federal giving runs straight into the
committee that holds it — not because he aimed it there, but because that's simply
where his long-standing incumbents sit. He doesn't need a conspiracy. He needs a
status quo. The data shows he's helping fund the rooms where the status quo is
kept.

## Methodology & provenance

- **Donations:** CONFIRMED + PROBABLE attributions to MLB principal owners in the
  CheckSwing FEC archive (`data/master.db`), each traceable to a specific FEC
  filing. Attribution rules: VERIFICATION.md (two-signal standard).
- **Crosswalk:** FEC candidate id → Bioguide id via the public-domain
  `unitedstates/congress-legislators` project (Tier-2 identification, SOURCES.md).
- **Bills & committees of referral:** Congress.gov API (bill identity, sponsors,
  and the `/committees` endpoint) — Tier-1 source of record. Bill lineage:
  S.1342 / H.R.811 (115th) → S.1192 / H.R.2434 (119th).
- **Committee membership:** `committee-membership-current.yaml` from
  `congress-legislators` — the **current-Congress** roster only. The committee
  join is fenced to bills of the current Congress (`committees.congress`) so a
  present-day member is never tied to a historical bill.
- **The join:** `python -m scripts.cli policy-join --via-committee 119-s-1192
  --via-committee 119-hr-2434 --sponsors-of 115-s-1342 --sponsors-of 115-hr-811
  --sponsors-of 119-s-1192 --sponsors-of 119-hr-2434 --out
  no-tax-subsidies-for-stadiums`. Reproducible neutral output in
  [`reports/data/`](data/).
- **External context** (the A's Las Vegas public financing) is widely reported
  public record; it is supporting color, not part of the neutral join, and is
  flagged as such.
- **Honesty note:** every donation figure above is from that output. Where the
  data undercuts a tempting narrative — no votes, current-roster timing, Fisher's
  two-person cluster, zero-to-authors — this brief leads with the undercut. That
  restraint is the product.
