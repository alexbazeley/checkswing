## The bit, up front

There is a federal law on the books called the **Save America's Pastime Act**. It
sounds like it protects baseball. What it actually does is strip minor-league
baseball players of their right to federal minimum-wage and overtime protection
under the Fair Labor Standards Act.

It was never debated on its own. It never got an up-or-down vote with its name on
it. It died quietly as a standalone bill in 2016 (H.R. 5580) — and then, in March
2018, its text reappeared inside the **2,232-page Consolidated Appropriations Act,
2018** (H.R. 1625), the must-pass omnibus spending bill that Congress had to
approve to keep the government open. It passed the House 256–167 and the Senate
65–32, was signed within days, and minor leaguers — who in many cases were earning
below minimum wage for a 60-hour work week — lost a legal argument they were
actually starting to win in court.

A bill named after *saving* the national pastime was, in practice, a bill to keep
the cost of the national pastime's labor force as low as the owners had always
paid it. That's the show in one sentence.

---

## Background: what SAPA did, and why it existed

- Minor-league players are employed by MLB clubs but paid a fraction of a living
  wage — historically a few hundred dollars a week, only during the season, with
  unpaid spring training and instructional time.
- A class of players sued (*Senne v. MLB*), arguing the FLSA entitled them to
  minimum wage and overtime. The suit had legs.
- MLB lobbied Congress for a statutory fix. The **Save America's Pastime Act**
  (H.R. 5580, 114th Congress) was that fix: amend the FLSA (29 U.S.C. §213) to
  carve minor leaguers out of minimum-wage and overtime coverage entirely.
- The standalone bill stalled amid public ridicule. The language was then inserted
  into the FY2018 omnibus (H.R. 1625, 115th Congress; Public Law 115-141) and
  enacted that way in March 2018.

This is baseball's labor politics in miniature: a coordinated ownership ask,
routed around a clean vote, attached to a bill no one could vote against, framed
with a patriotic name.

## The CheckSwing question — and the honest answer

CheckSwing exists to ask a specific question with receipts: **do the owners who
benefit from a law like this also fund the legislators who pass it?** Phase 3 of
the archive was built to answer exactly this — to join verified owner donations to
real roll-call votes.

So we ran it. We took every MLB principal owner in the archive, found their
verified federal donations to candidates, mapped each candidate to the legislator
who cast a vote on H.R. 1625 (the bill that carried SAPA), and lined the donations
up against the vote.

**The result is not a smoking gun. It's something more honest, and arguably worse.**

### What the data shows

- **10 owners** in the archive donated to legislators who voted on the SAPA carrier
  bill — **41 donations totaling $135,300.**
- The single biggest owner-donor in this set is **John Fisher of the Athletics —
  $31,900 across 11 donations** — the owner most synonymous with running baseball
  on the cheap.
- Next: **John Stanton (Mariners) $25,400**, **Ken Kendrick (Diamondbacks)
  $24,100**, **the Pohlads (Twins) ~$17,800 combined**, then Reinsdorf, Angelos,
  Cohen, Castellini, Sherman.

### What the data does *not* show — and we won't pretend otherwise

This is where the archive earns its keep. The neutral join refuses to support the
clean "owners bought this vote" story:

1. **The timing is wrong for a quid pro quo.** Of the 41 donations, **exactly one
   ($2,700) predates the March 2018 vote.** The other 40 — **$132,600** — came
   *after* it, spread from 2018 all the way to 2025. You cannot buy a 2018 vote
   with 2023 money.
2. **The money doesn't follow the vote.** Owner dollars split almost evenly between
   legislators who voted **Yea ($68,600)** and **Nay ($66,700)** on the carrier.
   If owners were rewarding the SAPA outcome, the money would lean Yea. It doesn't.
3. **The owners didn't fund the authors.** SAPA was written by **Rep. Brett Guthrie
   (R-KY)** and cosponsored by **Rep. Cheri Bustos (D-IL)**. Owner donations in the
   archive to either of them: **zero.**
4. **It looks like home-cooking, not policy-buying.** Fisher's entire $31,900 goes
   to a small cluster of House Democrats — Costa, Correa, and Panetta of California
   (his team's home state) plus Illinois's Brad Schneider — funded regardless of
   how they voted on the omnibus (he gave to both Yea and Nay voters in the group).
   It reads as incumbent-relationship maintenance, not a targeted purchase.

### The real story is structural, and that's the point

Here is the uncomfortable takeaway, and it's *stronger* than a bribe would have
been: **the owners didn't need to buy this vote.** Baseball's labor carve-outs —
the antitrust exemption, and now the minimum-wage exemption — are a settled,
bipartisan consensus in Washington. SAPA rode a must-pass omnibus to a 256–167 /
65–32 passage. Owner money in this dataset tilts, if anything, slightly **Democratic
($76,700 to D recipients vs. $58,600 to R)** — because it's chasing access to
whoever holds the seat, not enforcing a party-line outcome.

When the fix is already locked in on both sides of the aisle, you don't have to
pay for the vote. You pay for the relationship, year after year, so that the
consensus never moves. That's what the data actually shows: not a transaction, but
a standing arrangement.

## Talking points (for the show)

- **Lead with the name.** "The Save America's Pastime Act" stripped minor leaguers
  of minimum wage. Read the name twice. Let it sit.
- **The midnight-omnibus move.** It never got its own vote — it was slipped into a
  2,232-page must-pass spending bill. That's not an accident; that's the mechanism.
- **The Fisher line.** The owner most famous for paying for nothing is the biggest
  owner-donor in this set — $31,900 — though notably all of it to a handful of
  California Democrats and one Illinois cosponsor, not to SAPA's authors.
- **The honest twist (this is the rigorous-CheckSwing beat).** We looked for the
  bribe and didn't find it. The money mostly came *after* the vote and split
  evenly across Yea and Nay. The scarier truth: nobody had to be bought, because
  the carve-out is bipartisan and permanent.
- **Tie to the CBA.** This is the template for December 2026. The owners' federal
  strategy isn't buying single votes — it's maintaining a bipartisan consensus that
  treats baseball's labor costs as a settled question. Watch for the same move.

## Discussion questions

1. If SAPA had to get its own up-or-down vote, would it have passed? What does it
   say that it didn't get one?
2. Is a standing donor relationship that never has to ask for a specific vote more
   or less corrupting than a one-time quid pro quo?
3. Owner money here leaned Democratic. Does that complicate the "owners are just
   Republican megadonors" shorthand — or is access bipartisan by design?
4. *Senne v. MLB* was winding through the courts when Congress mooted it. When does
   lobbying to change the law stop being lobbying and start being a thumb on the
   scale of an active lawsuit?

## Methodology & provenance

- **Donations:** CONFIRMED + PROBABLE attributions to MLB principal owners in the
  CheckSwing FEC archive (`data/master.db`), each traceable to a specific FEC
  filing. Attribution rules: VERIFICATION.md (two-signal standard).
- **Crosswalk:** FEC candidate id → Bioguide id via the public-domain
  `unitedstates/congress-legislators` project (Tier-2 identification, SOURCES.md).
- **Bills & votes:** Congress.gov API (bill identity, sponsors) and the House Clerk
  EVS / Senate LIS roll-call XML (vote positions) — Tier-1 sources of record.
- **The join:** `python -m scripts.cli policy-join --bill 115-hr-1625
  --sponsors-of 114-hr-5580 --sponsors-of 115-hr-1625 --out save-americas-pastime-act`.
  Reproducible neutral output in [`reports/data/`](data/). `days_before_vote` is
  arithmetic, not a causal claim.
- **Honesty note:** every figure above is from that output. Where the data
  undercuts a tempting narrative (timing, Yea/Nay split, zero-to-authors), this
  brief reports the undercut rather than burying it. That restraint is the product.
